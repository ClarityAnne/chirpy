Well Met in Springtime

Grab the lantern. Enter a shady copse of MIDI, a veritable forest overgrown with digitally-produced sounds. These small files were once common on all sorts of independent websites. Now? You rarely see them anywhere. In fact, many sites disallow sharing them, and some browsers now find it difficult to play them. Welcome, for better or worse, to the MIDI Forest.

MIDI itself stands for musical instrument digital interface. This American-Japanese file format developed in the 1980s as a way to help machines understand musical instruments. Rather than transmitting actual audio, MIDI sends instructions. These include which note to play, how loud, for how long, and with what kind of expression (e.g., pitch bend, vibrato, sustain pedal).This makes MIDI massively flexible, enabling one small file to control entire orchestras of virtual instruments without storing any sound data itself.

MIDI created a universal language that allowed keyboards, synthesizers, drum machines, and digital audio workstations to interconnect seamlessly, regardless of brand. This standardization meant a musician could record a performance on a keyboard, edit it on a computer, and play it back using a completely different synth.

Today, MIDI is still a core part of modern music technology, even if it has fallen out of favor on the World Wide Web. At its core, MIDI remains precise, language-like system for telling machines how to think about music, not “what” the music sounds like. If audio is a recording of a singer’s voice, MIDI is the sheet music they’re reading from! And any instrument can interpret it, in infinite ways.

The green grove you now enter is a testimony to the ubiquity of MIDI in times past, as is the general aesthetic ambiance it partakes of. Websites in the Before Times often featured autoplaying MIDI files in the background. While this site (as of today) doesn’t have any autoplay, you can download plenty of MIDI from it, or just play them within your browser…
